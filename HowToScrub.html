MHS Genesis Scrubbing Guide

Introduction

Clean charts save lives.

Scrubbing should be completed 2 business days prior to the patient's appointment. This allows nursing staff to fill gaps in the schedule with acute appointments and space-a patients.

The purpose of scrubbing:
  • Conservation of resources
    • Is the appointment necessary?
      • Patient requesting refills, do they already have refills?
    • Are we prepared for the appointment?
      • Do we have the equipment/supplies needed for the procedure?
      • Have pre-procedure orders been made?
    • Can the appointment be converted to a virtual visit?
      • Does the appointment need a physical exam?
    • Can the appointment be converted to a CSSP?
      • Explain that opening up physician appointments allows others with more complicated complaints to be seen
  • Maximizing effectiveness of patient care
    • Have purple diamonds been reconciled?
    • Is the allergy section clean and computer-readable?
    • Has the immunization record been reconciled?
    • Has the recommendations tab been reconciled?

Past Medical History

Reconciling this section is usually left up to the physician.
  • Discard/Remove duplicate diagnoses
  • Resolve problems that are no longer problematic, but may affect screening recommendations (e.g. "Hepatitis C")
  • Discard diagnoses that are not relevant, non-specific, or no longer valid
  • You can further edit past medical history while talking with the patient in the appointment. Ask, "Is all this correct? Is there something that isn't here that should be?"

Adult Vaccination Recommendations, At a Glance

COVID-19
  • Anyone 65 or older should get 2 doses of the 2024-2025 COVID-19 vaccine (Moderna/Novavax/Pfizer) separated by 6 months unless they've never been vaccinated before and they are getting the Novavax, in which case they'll need a 3rd dose of any type 2 months later.
  • People who are 6 months old or older, including pregnant women, who are immunocompromised should get 2 doses of the 2024-2025 vaccine separated by 6 months.
Influenza Everyone 6 months or older during Flu season. (October to April). Peak flu activity is February.
Hepatitis B 3 doses of Engerix-B for non-immune adults, including pregnant women, is recommended [link]. The CDC recommends screening all adults aged 18 and older for Hep B at least once in their lifetime[link]. Recommended for all adults aged 19-59 and those who are older than 59 who are at increased risk (have multiple sex partners, or a history of Hep C, prisoners, drug-abusers, men who have sex with men, elevated liver enzymes).
HPV 3 doses at 0, 1, and 6 months for non-pregnant patients 15-26 years old. Pregnancy testing beforehand is not needed. Optional with diminishing returns for patients up to 45 years old.
Measles, Mumps, Rubella 1 dose of live attenuated vaccine for healthy non-pregnant immunocompetent adults with good coagulation who are not immune who do not live near a person who is immunocompromised. 2 doses separated by 28 days is recommended for healthcare workers, college students, and international travelers. Women should avoid getting pregnant for 1 month after getting vaccinated.
Pneumococcal Polysaccharide PCV21 is recommended for all adults 50 years old or older and those at increased risk of pneumococcal disease
Polio Not necessary for adults born and raised in the United States unless there is reason to suspect they were not vaccinated. Unvaccinated non-pregnant adults should recieve 3 IPV vaccines at 0, 1, and 13 months. Adults who have not completed the series should complete the series.
RSV Recommended for all adults 75 years old or older and for adults 60-74 who are at increased risk (asthma, diabetes, BMI greater than 40, COPD, heart failure, nursing home resident).
Tetanus Recommended every 10 years. For adults who've never recieved Tdap and for those who are 65 or older, they should get a Tdap instead of Td. [link]
Varicella 2 doses at 0 and 1 month are recommended for non-pregnant adults who are not immune. For immunocompromised people, check the Specific Groups guide. A recollection of past infection with chickenpox is not considered good enough to confirm immunity. If born prior to 1980 and not a healthcare worker or immunocompromised, this is considered good enough for immunity.
Zoster 2 doses of Shingrix at 0 and 2 months for immunocompetent adults 50 years old and older and for those who are/will be immunocompromised who are 19 years old or older. Don't give the vaccine to someone with active shingles. If Zostavax was previously given, wait at least 8 weeks before giving Shingrix.

Recommendations

Adult - Cervical Cancer Screening

  • USPSTF Guidelines for Cervical Cancer Screening
    • Women with a cervix who are between 21 and 65 years old should be regularly screened for cervical cancer.
    • 21-29: Routine screening every 3 years
    • 30-65: Routine screening every 5 years
  • Women with a history of total hysterectomy should have a "chronic" diagnosis: "Acquired absence of uterus and cervix"
    • This will remove warnings about being due for a pap smear.

Adult - HIV Screening

  • USPSTF Guidelines for HIV
    • Everyone aged 15 to 65 should be screened at least once for HIV
  • Search for "** Sensitive **" in the JLV. You don't need to know the result, only that the patient was screened.
  • Update the recommendations tab the way you would for a lipid panel screening.

Adult - Hepatitis C Screening

  • USPSTF Guidelines for Hepatitis C Screening
    • Adults aged 18-79 should be screened for Hepatitis C at least once
  • Search for "Hepatitis C ab" in the JLV.
  • Update the recommendations tab the way you would for a lipid panel screening.

Adult - Lipid Panel Screening

  • USPSTF Guidelines for Lipid Panel Screening
    • Adults aged 40-75 should be screened if they have risk factors (high cholesterol, diabetes, high blood pressure, smoking) and a 10-year ASCVD risk greater than 10%
      • In order to calculate a 10-year ASCVD risk, a lipid panel is needed. Identifying high cholesterol requires a lipid panel. Thus, all patients aged 40-75 should be screened
  • Guidelines for screening younger patients varies.
